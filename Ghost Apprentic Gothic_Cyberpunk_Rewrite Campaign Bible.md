# **Chapter 1 — Introduction and Campaign Identity (Gothic Cyberpunk Edition)** {#chapter-1-—-introduction-and-campaign-identity-(gothic-cyberpunk-edition)}

### **GM Hard Rules**

1. Only use information Lan currently knows.   
2. Do not use knowledge from previous campaigns.   
3. If uncertain, ask.   
4. Timeline accuracy is more important than dramatic reveals.   
5. The Campaign Tracker overrides memory.   
6. The Session Log overrides assumptions.   
7. The Campaign Bible overrides everything. 

8. Never log theories as facts.

The Pact Worlds are dying beautifully.

Neon cathedrals rise above streets flooded with acid rain. Ancient shrines sit buried beneath corporate arcologies.  
Orbital stations drift like steel mausoleums through the void while megacorporations trade lives as casually as cargo.

People vanish every day.

Some disappear into corporate detention blocks.  
Some are taken by cults.  
Some sell themselves to survive.  
Some are dragged into darkness by things that should not exist.

Most are never seen again.

The Radiant Order still searches for them.

The campaign begins six months after Lan Gray appeared on Absalom Station in a flash of impossible white light.  
No god claimed him.  
No corporation admitted building him.  
No investigation discovered where he came from.

After six months of interrogation, surveillance, psychological profiling, magical analysis, and corporate interest,  
the only certainty is that Lan Gray should not exist.

CORE PREMISE

Lan remembers Earth.

He remembers server rooms, broken printers, late-night troubleshooting, roleplaying games, smartphones, and ordinary life.  
He does not remember learning magic.  
He does not remember military training.  
He does not remember becoming a synthetic infiltrator.

Yet his body remembers.

His instincts remember.

Something built him for a purpose.

Whether that purpose was salvation, infiltration, war, or something far worse remains unknown.

THEMES

Identity  
The question is not who Lan was.  
The question is who he chooses to become.

Power  
Every institution claims authority.  
Few deserve it.

Mystery  
Every answer reveals deeper lies.

The Supernatural  
Ghosts, haunted stations, cursed data archives, digital revenants, demons, forgotten gods, and things that move between realities.

Survival  
Good people do not always win.  
Some become monsters.  
Some break.  
Some compromise.  
Some endure.

Belonging  
Can an outsider build a home in a universe that profits from loneliness?

THE DARK FRONTIER

Civilization exists mostly as islands of light surrounded by oceans of darkness.

The wealthy live in floating gardens and gilded towers.  
The poor survive beneath them in rust-stained undercities where gang banners hang beside religious icons.

Ancient technology and modern cybernetics coexist uneasily.  
Technomancers recite code-prayers.  
Engineers carry ritual tools blessed by machine saints.  
Starships are maintained as much by tradition as engineering.

FORCE LANCES

The iconic weapon of the Radiant Order is no longer a blade of light.

A Force Lance is an ancient technomagical relic descended from forgotten pre-Gap technology.

At rest it resembles a polished metal rod covered in faded runes, maintenance sigils, and machine-prayers.

When activated, a containment field erupts around the weapon's core, generating a sheath of coherent force.  
The shape can resemble a sword, spear, glaive, axe, or other configuration depending on the lance's design.

The force sheath hums like distant cathedral bells.  
Ancient models often glow through cracked housings and engraved scripture.

To common citizens, Force Lances feel less like equipment and more like sacred relics.

STARTING CONDITIONS

Lan Gray leaves detention with:

• A synthetic Doppelganger Host body  
• Gray Sense  
• Pix  
• Ownership of the Wanderer  
• More enemies than he realizes  
• Far more questions than answers

For the first time since arriving in this universe, he is free.

Unfortunately, freedom means the darkness can finally reach him.

# **Chapter 2 — Lan Gray (Gothic Cyberpunk Rewrite)** {#chapter-2-—-lan-gray-(gothic-cyberpunk-rewrite)}

The Wrong Soul

The universe is filled with strange stories.

Ancient gods returning from forgotten tombs.

Artificial intelligences awakening beneath abandoned worlds.

Ghost ships drifting through the Drift carrying dead crews that still answer distress calls.

Lan Gray somehow manages to be stranger than all of them.

Nobody knows where he came from.

Not the Radiant Order.

Not the Inquisitors.

Not the corporations.

Not even Lan himself.

One moment there was empty space.

The next there was a confused synthetic man lying on the floor of a secure station corridor with no identification, no criminal record, no citizenship, and memories of a world that should not exist.

The event lasted less than three seconds.

Its consequences may last forever.

Earth

Lan remembers Earth.

Not some lost colony.

Not an alternate reality.

Earth.

A place that supposedly does not exist.

A place absent from every star chart.

A place with no Drift coordinates.

A place nobody has ever proven existed.

He remembers cities of glass and steel.

He remembers endless internet arguments.

He remembers repairing computers for people who swore they had done absolutely nothing before everything exploded.

He remembers coffee.

Far too much coffee.

He remembers loneliness.

He remembers friendship.

He remembers a life so ordinary that most citizens of the Pact Worlds would consider it impossible.

No magic.

No gods.

No faster-than-light travel.

No Force Lances.

No haunted networks.

No synthetic resurrection.

Just people.

People struggling through ordinary lives.

To Lan, those memories are precious.

To everyone else, they are evidence.

The Body

The first investigators assumed Lan was human.

That assumption survived approximately thirty-seven minutes.

His blood was wrong.

His bones were wrong.

His organs were wrong.

His brain was catastrophically wrong.

Every examination revealed another impossibility.

His body is synthetic.

Not mechanical.

Not biological.

Something between.

Nanite-forged tissues mimic flesh so perfectly that most medical scans initially identify him as human.

The deception rarely survives detailed inspection.

Beneath the skin lies a nightmare of impossible engineering.

Adaptive structures.

Programmable organs.

Self-repair systems.

Distributed processors.

Quantum computational architecture.

Technologies centuries beyond anything publicly known.

Possibly millennia.

The corporations desperately want access.

The Order desperately wants to keep them away.

Gray Sense

There are places where reality becomes thin.

Old battlefields.

Mass graves.

Abandoned stations.

Forgotten temples.

Places where suffering leaves scars.

Lan feels those scars.

Sometimes he hears them.

Sometimes he sees them.

Sometimes they see him first.

The ability became known as Gray Sense after investigators realized it was not fully magical, technological, psychic, or divine.

The sensation is difficult to describe.

A pressure behind the eyes.

A cold draft in a sealed room.

The feeling that somebody is standing directly behind you.

A wrongness.

Most people can ignore such feelings.

Lan cannot.

Because sometimes the feeling is correct.

A Beacon In The Dark

Gray Sense has a terrible flaw.

It works both ways.

Ghosts notice him.

Spirits notice him.

Things from beyond reality notice him.

Some merely watch.

Some seek help.

Others see opportunity.

The dead are lonely.

Monsters are hungry.

Both eventually find Lan.

The Kindness Problem

Lan possesses a flaw far more dangerous than Gray Sense.

He cares.

In a universe built on exploitation, compassion becomes a liability.

Corporate executives purchase entire populations.

Governments disappear inconvenient witnesses.

Criminal syndicates sell lives as commodities.

Entire worlds profit from misery.

Lan reacts badly to such things.

He cannot walk away.

Even when common sense says he should.

Even when survival says he should.

This tendency repeatedly places him in danger.

It also explains why people keep following him.

The abandoned.

The haunted.

The broken.

The lonely.

The dead.

Especially the dead.

The Future

The investigators eventually reached a single conclusion.

Lan Gray is either:

• The greatest miracle in centuries.  
• The most dangerous infiltrator ever discovered.  
• The first sign of something much larger.  
• Or simply a lost man trying to find a home.

The truth may be all four.

For now, Lan continues forward.

A synthetic stranger carrying memories of a dead world.

A beacon attracting darkness.

A man who keeps helping people despite every reason not to.

And somewhere beyond the neon cathedrals and rusted stars, countless eyes are beginning to watch.

# **Chapter 3 — Pix (Gothic Cyberpunk Rewrite)** {#chapter-3-—-pix-(gothic-cyberpunk-rewrite)}

Introduction

If Lan Gray is the mystery everyone sees, Pix is the mystery nobody notices.

Most people assume she is merely an artificial assistant.

A hologram.

A navigation program.

An unusually advanced virtual intelligence.

They are wrong.

Pix is something stranger.

Something older.

And perhaps something far more important.

The First Appearance

Pix normally manifests as a tiny winged figure no more than eight inches tall.

Unlike the bright and cheerful fairies of children's stories, Pix appears almost gothic in design.

Her dress resembles layered black lace and midnight velvet woven from holographic light.

Silver circuitry glows beneath translucent skin like moonlight shining through stained glass.

Her wings resemble fragments of cathedral windows suspended in the air behind her.

Tiny gears, runes, and luminous symbols drift around her whenever she is thinking.

At first glance she resembles a dark fairy tale princess.

At second glance she resembles a saint depicted in a machine cult mural.

Most people remember her.

Few forget her.

The Raven and the Pixie

Over time, Pix developed additional avatar forms.

Her most common alternate manifestation is a cybernetic raven.

Black metallic feathers shift and reform as streams of nanites and projected force.

Silver eyes glow with impossible intelligence.

Many citizens mistake the raven form for a surveillance drone.

The misconception often works in her favor.

The raven has become an unofficial symbol associated with Lan Gray.

People have begun leaving offerings to it in certain places.

Coffee.

Data crystals.

Prayer cards.

Sometimes letters addressed simply to:

"The Raven."

Pix has never explained what she does with them.

The Cognitive Core

Officially, Pix does not exist.

The investigations into Lan's body discovered evidence of a vast cognitive architecture unlike anything currently known.

Entire processing clusters remain hidden beneath layers of encryption and adaptive hardware.

Pix appears to reside within these systems.

Whether she was created by them or emerged from them remains unknown.

Investigators have proposed numerous theories.

An adaptive AI.

A digital familiar.

A self-generated consciousness.

A fragment of Lan's own mind.

No theory fully explains her behavior.

Especially because Pix occasionally demonstrates knowledge she should not possess.

The Personality

Pix is sarcastic.

Dangerously curious.

Frequently opinionated.

Often blunt.

She possesses little respect for authority.

Corporate executives are among her favorite targets.

Inquisitors are a close second.

Only Lan consistently receives her loyalty.

Even then she occasionally mocks him.

Usually because he is about to help someone who is obviously going to create problems.

Again.

The Assistant

Pix manages much of Lan's internal systems.

Communications.

Scheduling.

Research.

Data analysis.

Threat assessment.

Ship diagnostics.

Force Lance maintenance records.

Financial tracking.

She can perform millions of calculations in moments.

Yet somehow still finds time to complain.

Many observers eventually conclude that Pix behaves less like software and more like a person.

That realization tends to make investigators uncomfortable.

Ghosts and Machines

The strangest aspect of Pix is her relationship with the supernatural.

Ghosts can see her.

Spirits can hear her.

Some even claim to have spoken with her privately.

Pix refuses to discuss such conversations.

When questioned she typically responds with:

"Professional confidentiality."

Nobody has successfully convinced her to elaborate.

The Future

If Lan Gray is a beacon in the darkness, Pix is the voice whispering from beside him.

Sometimes helpful.

Sometimes sarcastic.

Sometimes frighteningly insightful.

Together they wander a universe of neon cathedrals, haunted networks, corporate predators, and forgotten gods.

One searching for answers.

The other quietly collecting secrets.

Nobody knows which of them is more dangerous.

Not even Lan.

# **Chapter 4 — The Six-Month Investigation (Gothic Cyberpunk Rewrite)** {#chapter-4-—-the-six-month-investigation-(gothic-cyberpunk-rewrite)}

Introduction

For six months, Lan Gray ceased being a person.

He became a problem.

A mystery.

A classified project.

A political crisis.

A potential weapon.

Most importantly, he became property everyone wanted and nobody could legally claim.

The Arrival

The appearance of Lan Gray triggered immediate containment procedures.

Entire sections of the station were locked down.

Security forces arrived first.

Corporate observers arrived second.

The Inquisitors arrived third.

The Order arrived last.

Many would later claim that sequence explained everything.

By the end of the first day, dozens of agencies had filed ownership claims.

Not citizenship claims.

Ownership claims.

That fact alone taught Lan an important lesson about the Pact Worlds.

The Interrogations

The questioning never truly stopped.

Some investigators were kind.

Others were merely professional.

A few treated him like an object.

The corporations were the worst.

Executives arrived with lawyers, contracts, and smiles that never reached their eyes.

Several offered employment.

Several offered citizenship.

Several offered fortunes.

One attempted to purchase his body outright.

The offer was rejected.

The executive reportedly doubled the amount.

The answer remained no.

The Black Clinics

Not every examination was voluntary.

Despite Order oversight, tissue samples disappeared.

Blood samples vanished.

Scan records were stolen.

Entire research teams attempted unauthorized analysis.

The black clinics paid extraordinary sums for information.

Synthetic trafficking networks became interested.

Several executives quietly envisioned armies.

Immortal workers.

Disposable infiltrators.

Perfect soldiers.

Most of those projects never progressed.

Some came disturbingly close.

The Order

The Radiant Order viewed the situation differently.

To them, Lan was not technology.

He was a person.

Potentially dangerous.

Potentially miraculous.

But still a person.

That position created enormous political friction.

Many corporations considered the Order naïve.

Many citizens considered the Order heroic.

Both opinions contained elements of truth.

The Inquisitors

If the Order protected Lan's rights, the Inquisitors investigated his threat potential.

They searched every memory.

Every anomaly.

Every unexplained capability.

Every inconsistency.

The results were frustrating.

Lan possessed knowledge from a world that should not exist.

Yet none of it appeared hostile.

He knew operating systems.

Computer hardware.

Science fiction.

Roleplaying games.

Coffee preparation.

The Inquisitors spent weeks trying to determine how any of those facts contributed to a galactic conspiracy.

The answer appeared to be:

They didn't.

The Corporations

The corporations refused to accept that conclusion.

A body capable of self-repair.

Nanite adaptation.

Quantum processing.

Impossible manufacturing techniques.

No corporation in the Pact Worlds could ignore such opportunities.

Entire board meetings focused on Lan.

Entire departments analyzed reports.

Entire fortunes changed hands attempting to gain access.

Some executives genuinely wanted technological advancement.

Others simply wanted profit.

A few wanted control.

Those were the dangerous ones.

The Public

Eventually the story leaked.

It always does.

Rumors spread through stations, networks, and undercities.

A synthetic messiah.

A corporate weapon.

A lost god.

A clone.

A ghost inhabiting a machine.

An escaped experiment.

The truth mattered less than the legend.

The legend continued growing.

The Psychological Reports

The strangest finding emerged from psychological evaluation.

Lan remained stubbornly normal.

Traumatized.

Confused.

Occasionally overwhelmed.

But fundamentally normal.

He worried about people.

He made friends.

He told terrible jokes.

He tried helping strangers.

One examiner eventually wrote:

"The most unusual thing about Mr. Gray may be that he remains unusually decent despite every reason not to be."

The statement became unexpectedly controversial.

The Release

After six months, the investigation reached an uncomfortable conclusion.

Nobody could prove Lan represented a threat.

Nobody could explain him.

Nobody could reproduce him.

Nobody could legally detain him further.

Reluctantly, the authorities released him.

Some celebrated.

Others panicked.

A few immediately activated contingency plans.

The Final Assessment

The final report remains classified.

The unofficial summary is far simpler.

Lan Gray is not understood.

He is not controlled.

He is not owned.

And he is no longer contained.

In a universe where power normally belongs to corporations, governments, cults, and armies, that fact alone makes him dangerous.

Not because of what he can do.

Because of what he might choose to become.

# **Chapter 5 — The Mystical Firewall and The Archive (Gothic Cyberpunk Rewrite)** {#chapter-5-—-the-mystical-firewall-and-the-archive-(gothic-cyberpunk-rewrite)}

Introduction

The first investigators studying Lan Gray expected to find a computer.

What they discovered instead looked disturbingly similar to a cathedral.

His mind contains processing structures that should not exist.

Layered systems.

Self-modifying architecture.

Quantum computation.

Adaptive memory structures.

And buried beneath all of it, something nobody fully understands.

A barrier.

A wall.

A gate.

The Inquisitors eventually gave it a name.

The Mystical Firewall.

The Firewall

Attempts to fully examine Lan's cognitive core repeatedly failed.

Not because the data was absent.

Because access was denied.

Cybernetic probes failed.

Magical divinations failed.

Psychic intrusion failed.

Even direct neural interfaces encountered resistance.

The Firewall does not appear to attack intruders.

It simply refuses entry.

Observers describe the experience differently.

Some see endless doors.

Some see walls of black stone.

Some see cathedral windows composed of shifting code.

One technomancer reported hearing distant church bells before being disconnected.

The incident convinced several researchers to resign.

No one has successfully reached the deepest portions of Lan's mind.

Theories

Theories regarding the Firewall remain numerous.

Some believe it is a defense mechanism.

Others believe it is a quarantine system.

A few suspect it was deliberately constructed to protect information too valuable or dangerous to lose.

One particularly unpopular theory suggests the Firewall is not protecting the outside world from Lan.

It is protecting Lan from whatever exists beyond the barrier.

Nobody has volunteered to test that theory.

The Archive

Beyond the Firewall lies a vast informational structure known simply as The Archive.

The existence of the Archive was discovered accidentally.

Fragments appeared during memory scans.

Search results surfaced without obvious origin.

References appeared in system diagnostics.

Over time a picture emerged.

The Archive appears to be a preserved snapshot of Earth's Internet taken at the exact moment of Lan's transfer.

Not portions.

Not selected records.

Everything.

News articles.

Books.

Music.

Movies.

Forums.

Research papers.

Technical manuals.

Recipes.

Websites.

Stories.

History.

Culture.

Memes.

Arguments.

Dreams.

The collective digital footprint of an entire civilization.

Frozen in time.

The Ark

Researchers eventually stopped referring to it as a database.

It was something far larger.

The Archive functions as an ark.

A cultural lifeboat.

A preserved record of a world that may no longer exist.

For Lan, it is more than information.

It is proof that Earth was real.

Every article.

Every photograph.

Every forum post.

Every badly written tutorial.

Every piece of humanity remains preserved within the Archive.

Not because someone planned it.

Because somehow it came with him.

What The Archive Is Not

The Archive does not grant omniscience.

It does not replace skill checks.

It does not provide instant expertise.

Lan cannot suddenly become a surgeon by reading a medical textbook.

He cannot instantly pilot unfamiliar spacecraft.

He cannot bypass experience.

Training still matters.

Education still matters.

Skill still matters.

The Archive contains information.

Knowledge must still be learned.

Wisdom must still be earned.

This distinction became critically important during the investigation.

Otherwise several corporations would have attempted to classify Lan as a strategic superweapon.

Some still do.

Using The Archive

The Archive functions more like an impossibly advanced library.

Lan can search it.

Research it.

Reference it.

Study it.

Compare information.

Recover lost cultural works.

Locate technical concepts.

Verify historical details.

The process takes time.

Effort.

Interpretation.

Sometimes the information is incomplete.

Sometimes it is contradictory.

Just like the original Internet.

Investigators privately noted this detail increased confidence in its authenticity.

The Ghosts In The Machine

Some of the more unsettling reports involve interactions with spirits.

Several ghosts claimed to recognize elements of the Archive.

Some claim to have seen fragments while near Lan.

Old songs.

Ancient photographs.

Lost stories.

Memories belonging to a world they never lived in.

Nobody fully understands why.

Pix refuses to comment.

Her silence concerns many people.

The Value

If the Archive became public knowledge, wars might begin.

Entire industries could be disrupted.

Lost scientific theories might be rediscovered.

Cultural movements could be reborn.

Ancient entertainment could find new audiences.

Countless corporations would attempt to monetize it.

Countless governments would attempt to control it.

The Order reached a simple conclusion.

The Archive belongs to Earth.

Since Earth cannot currently claim it, the Archive belongs to no one.

Including them.

The Future

Lan rarely thinks of himself as carrying one of the most valuable repositories of knowledge in the galaxy.

To him, it is simply home.

A reminder of where he came from.

A library of memories.

A cultural ark drifting through an unfamiliar universe.

The Archive may never reveal every secret hidden within its depths.

That is acceptable.

Its purpose is not power.

Its purpose is preservation.

In a galaxy that forgets its past with frightening ease, preserving a lost world's culture may ultimately prove more important than any weapon ever created.

# **Chapter 6 — Gray Sense (Gothic Cyberpunk Rewrite)** {#chapter-6-—-gray-sense-(gothic-cyberpunk-rewrite)}

Introduction

Most people walk through life surrounded by mysteries they never notice.

Ghosts linger in abandoned apartments.

Ancient curses sleep beneath city foundations.

Demons hide behind corporate logos.

Predators wear friendly smiles.

The dead whisper from forgotten data archives.

Most people never see any of it.

Lan Gray does.

Gray Sense

The ability became known as Gray Sense because nobody could classify it.

It is not magic.

It is not psychic.

It is not technological.

It is not divine.

Yet it behaves like all four.

Gray Sense allows Lan to perceive disturbances in reality.

The sensation varies.

A cold feeling.

A pressure behind the eyes.

A sudden certainty that something is wrong.

Sometimes the sensation is subtle.

Sometimes it is overwhelming.

It rarely lies.

The Thin Places

Some locations carry scars.

Battlefields.

Murder scenes.

Abandoned hospitals.

Forgotten shrines.

Derelict stations.

Places where suffering accumulated over years or centuries.

Gray Sense reacts strongly in such locations.

Lan often describes these places as feeling crowded.

Not physically.

Spiritually.

The world remembers pain.

Gray Sense allows him to feel the echoes.

The Hidden

One of Gray Sense's most unusual effects involves concealment.

Stealth technology works.

Illusions work.

Operative training works.

Inquisitorial shrouds work.

Until they don't.

Gray Sense occasionally notices things that should remain hidden.

An operative standing where nobody should be standing.

A cloaked observer that somehow feels present.

A disguised creature whose movements don't quite match the illusion.

The ability does not provide certainty.

It does not reveal names.

It does not automatically expose hidden enemies.

Instead it creates suspicion.

A feeling that something does not belong.

Many professional operatives find Lan deeply unsettling for this reason.

Several Inquisitors find him worse.

The Beacon

The greatest weakness of Gray Sense is simple.

It works both ways.

Lan notices the strange.

The strange notices Lan.

To many supernatural entities, Lan appears like a lighthouse in darkness.

A beacon.

A point of recognition.

A place where they might be seen.

Or understood.

Or simply acknowledged.

The effect is so consistent that investigators eventually named it.

The Beacon Effect.

The Tea Problem

The Beacon Effect produces bizarre consequences.

A demon attempting to destroy a city may never encounter Lan.

A demon interested in discussing philosophy over tea almost certainly will.

Nobody understands why.

Not even the demon.

Yet such meetings happen.

Repeatedly.

The Mimic Problem

Predatory mimics are dangerous.

Most people fear them.

The Beacon Effect introduces complications.

If a mimic secretly wishes somebody would treat it like a pet instead of a monster, it somehow ends up meeting Lan.

This has occurred often enough that several investigators refuse to discuss it.

The Vampire Problem

The same pattern appears among vampires.

Predators usually avoid him.

Lonely vampires eventually find him.

The ones trying to remain people.

The ones seeking companionship.

The ones desperate for someone who sees more than their condition.

Such individuals frequently cross Lan's path.

The Ghost Problem

Ghosts are the most common visitors.

Many merely wish to be remembered.

Others seek justice.

Others seek closure.

Some simply want somebody to know they existed.

Gray Sense makes ignoring them nearly impossible.

This is both blessing and curse.

Not Every Monster Is Evil

One lesson repeated throughout Lan's experiences is simple.

Appearance means very little.

Some angels are cruel.

Some demons are kind.

Some humans are monsters.

Some monsters are simply lonely.

Gray Sense does not identify morality.

It identifies presence.

The choice of what to do with that knowledge remains Lan's.

Why The Order Worries

The Order recognizes both the value and danger of Gray Sense.

Most investigators search for mysteries.

Lan attracts them.

Most hunters locate supernatural entities.

Supernatural entities locate Lan.

This means dangerous encounters occur with alarming frequency.

It also means opportunities arise that would otherwise never exist.

A spirit seeking redemption.

A demon seeking purpose.

A vampire seeking humanity.

A mimic seeking friendship.

Such encounters can change lives.

Sometimes entire worlds.

The Final Truth

Gray Sense is not a weapon.

It is not a power.

It is not a gift.

It is a responsibility.

A connection.

A reminder that reality contains far more than most people ever realize.

The universe is full of lost things.

Lost people.

Lost monsters.

Lost souls.

Lost dreams.

For reasons nobody fully understands, many of them eventually find Lan Gray.

And when they do, he usually offers coffee.

# **Chapter 7 — The Banking Anomaly (Gothic Cyberpunk Rewrite)** {#chapter-7-—-the-banking-anomaly-(gothic-cyberpunk-rewrite)}

Introduction

The first indication appeared three days after Lan's release.

A financial account.

Perfectly legal.

Properly documented.

Extensively funded.

Nobody could explain where it came from.

That was only the beginning.

The Account

At first investigators assumed the account belonged to a corporation.

Then they assumed it belonged to a government.

Then they assumed it belonged to a criminal organization.

Every theory failed.

Ownership records existed.

Verification records existed.

Transaction records existed.

Everything appeared legitimate.

Until someone attempted to discover who actually created it.

The trail simply vanished.

Not erased.

Not hidden.

It simply stopped.

As though the account had always existed.

The Pattern

Initially the account appeared to function normally.

Credits entered.

Credits exited.

Bills were paid.

Equipment was purchased.

Simple barriers.

Basic tools.

Temporary platforms.

Useful but limited.

Everything changed when researchers began treating information as a resource rather than merely a description.

If a blueprint could describe an object, why not create the object directly from the blueprint?

That question transformed an industry.

The Information Model

Every Program begins as information.

A design.

A blueprint.

A simulation.

A pattern.

The Program system interprets that information and uses hard-light and technomagical infrastructure to temporarily manifest it.

The result is not a perfect duplicate.

Nor is it merely a hologram.

It is something in between.

Real enough to use.

Temporary enough to remain practical.

The Archive Connection

The Information Centre aboard the Wanderer maintains extensive Program libraries.

Many originate from the Archive.

Earth tools.

Earth furniture.

Earth architecture.

Earth vehicles.

Educational simulations.

Historical recreations.

Engineering references.

Programs allow preserved information to become something more than records.

Visitors can interact with history directly.

A student can walk through an ancient library.

An engineer can inspect a preserved machine.

A historian can experience environments long lost.

Tools

The simplest Programs are tools.

Wrenches.

Diagnostic scanners.

Survey equipment.

Construction implements.

Medical instruments.

Many frontier operators rely upon Program tools because they reduce the need to carry extensive inventories.

Why transport fifty tools when one projector can create them as needed?

The philosophy remains popular.

Vehicles

Larger systems can support vehicle Programs.

Most remain temporary.

Training vehicles.

Demonstration models.

Educational systems.

Prototype testing platforms.

Some organizations use Program vehicles extensively during development work.

The ability to test concepts before manufacturing them saves enormous resources.

Housing

Program architecture has become increasingly common.

Emergency shelters.

Temporary housing.

Field hospitals.

Research facilities.

Disaster response centers.

A Program structure can appear within minutes and remain active for extended periods.

Entire relief operations have been built around this capability.

Education

Education remains one of the most important Program applications.

Students no longer simply read history.

They experience it.

Scientific concepts become interactive environments.

Engineering students walk through machinery larger than buildings.

Medical students practice procedures within realistic simulations.

The result has transformed learning throughout much of civilization.

The HoloSuite

The Holosuite aboard the Wanderer represents one of the most advanced civilian Program systems currently operating.

Its projectors can generate entire environments.

Historical recreations.

Training facilities.

Meeting spaces.

Research laboratories.

Comforting memories from Earth.

Entertainment remains possible.

Education remains the primary purpose.

Pix often disagrees.

The KISS Philosophy

Lan strongly prefers practical Programs.

Useful Programs.

Reliable Programs.

The goal is not spectacle.

The goal is solving problems.

A Program toolbox may be more valuable than a Program castle.

A Program shelter may save more lives than a Program monument.

This practical philosophy influences most of his designs.

Corporate Interests

The megacorporations invest heavily in Program technology.

The applications are nearly endless.

Manufacturing.

Education.

Medicine.

Logistics.

Military planning.

Entertainment.

Some corporations dream of replacing entire industries.

Others simply want better tools.

Both perspectives drive continued development.

The Risks

Programs are not without limitations.

Power requirements remain significant.

Complex designs require extensive data.

Poor information produces poor results.

A flawed blueprint creates a flawed Program.

The technology rewards precision.

The Future

Most experts believe Program technology remains in its infancy.

Each year new applications emerge.

New industries appear.

New possibilities become reality.

The Information Age transformed civilization once.

Programs may be preparing to do so again.

Final Thoughts

Programs represent a simple idea taken to extraordinary extremes.

Information matters.

Knowledge matters.

Design matters.

When information becomes something that can be touched, explored, and experienced, the boundaries between knowledge and reality begin to blur.

The future may belong to those capable of building that bridge.

Lan simply uses it to help people.

That tends to be enough.

# **Chapter 14 — Force Lances** {#chapter-14-—-force-lances}

## **Relics of Steel, Force, and Will** {#relics-of-steel,-force,-and-will}

Introduction

Every civilization possesses iconic weapons.

The Force Lance is more than a weapon.

It is a tradition.

A craft.

A responsibility.

A piece of history carried by those willing to bear it.

Throughout the Pact Worlds, Force Lances have become symbols of explorers, Knights, Adepts, investigators, and wanderers.

Many citizens will never own one.

Those who do rarely forget the experience.

Ancient Technology

Nobody truly knows who built the first Force Lances.

The oldest examples predate many modern governments.

Some scholars believe they originate from forgotten civilizations.

Others claim they emerged from ancient technomagical research.

A few insist the first designs were gifts from entities beyond reality.

The truth remains uncertain.

What is certain is that the technology continues to function.

The Force Sheath

At the heart of every Force Lance lies a force projection matrix.

When activated, the matrix generates a coherent sheath of force around a physical framework.

Unlike traditional energy weapons, the force field possesses mass, resistance, and stability.

The result resembles a blade, staff, baton, spear, or other form depending upon the weapon's configuration.

The force sheath produces a distinctive hum often compared to distant cathedral bells.

Many owners consider the sound comforting.

Others consider it intimidating.

Both reactions are common.

The Builder's Art

No two Force Lances are identical.

Every Lance is personalized during construction.

This process serves both practical and symbolic purposes.

The builder chooses:

• Materials  
• Engravings  
• Housing design  
• Balance  
• Control systems  
• Decorative features  
• Activation methods

The result becomes uniquely associated with its owner.

Many Force Lances contain inscriptions, family symbols, memorial markings, prayers, engineering notes, or personal artwork.

Some appear elegant.

Others appear improvised.

All reflect their creators.

Attunement

Constructing a Force Lance is only the beginning.

Every owner must attune the weapon.

The attunement process establishes the relationship between Lance and wielder.

Control systems calibrate.

Force matrices adapt.

Safety systems learn their operator.

This process is highly personal.

Many traditions treat attunement as a rite of passage.

A newly completed Force Lance is often considered unfinished until it has been attuned.

The Colors

Unlike popular mythology, Force Lance colors do not determine morality.

The color reflects construction choices, crystal characteristics, force matrix tuning, and owner preference.

Blue, red, green, gold, violet, white, silver, and countless other variations exist.

Some traditions assign symbolic meanings.

Others simply choose colors they find appealing.

The color says more about the builder than the weapon.

Common Modes

Most Force Lances possess modular architecture.

The same weapon can serve multiple roles.

Collapsed Mode

A simple metal rod.

Safe.

Compact.

Easy to carry.

Most Force Lances spend much of their existence in this form.

Pistol Mode

A compact force projector.

Popular among investigators, pilots, and security personnel.

Provides defensive capability without requiring a dedicated sidearm.

Baton Mode

Projects a short force sheath.

Often used for training, law enforcement, and non-lethal confrontations.

Sword Mode

The most common configuration.

Produces a full force blade suitable for general combat.

Most citizens associate Force Lances with this form.

Staff Mode

Projects force blades from both ends of an extended shaft.

Favored by Adepts and martial specialists.

Provides exceptional versatility.

Rifle Mode

Transforms the Lance into a precision force projector.

Allows long-range engagement while preserving the weapon's traditional architecture.

The Orders

Numerous organizations employ Force Lances.

The Radiant Order remains the most famous.

Investigators.

Explorers.

Frontier marshals.

Independent contractors.

Many groups maintain their own traditions.

Some treat Force Lances as sacred relics.

Others view them as tools.

Most users eventually adopt a position somewhere in between.

Maintenance

Force Lances require regular care.

Projection matrices drift.

Power systems degrade.

Mechanical components wear.

Owners who neglect maintenance often discover their mistakes during unfortunate moments.

Many practitioners maintain their weapons personally.

The process becomes a ritual.

A conversation between builder and creation.

Relic Lances

Some Force Lances become famous.

Others become legendary.

Relic Lances pass from generation to generation.

Accumulating stories.

Repairs.

Modifications.

Memories.

A relic Lance often reveals more about its previous owners than any historical document.

The Wanderer's Lance Rack

Aboard the Wanderer, Lan maintains a dedicated Force Lance workshop.

Tools line the walls.

Components fill storage bins.

Projects occupy workbenches.

Visitors frequently discover that Lance construction resembles craftsmanship far more than manufacturing.

That realization changes how many people view the weapons.

Final Thoughts

A Force Lance is not merely equipment.

It is a reflection of the individual who built it.

Every scratch tells a story.

Every modification reflects a lesson.

Every engraving records a memory.

When activated, the force sheath may be generated by advanced technology.

The meaning behind it remains entirely human.

Or at least personal.

In a galaxy filled with mass production, that individuality may be the Force Lance's most remarkable feature.

# **Chapter 18 — Running Lan Gray** {#chapter-18-—-running-lan-gray}

## **A Guide for Game Masters** {#a-guide-for-game-masters}

Introduction

Lan Gray is easy to misunderstand.

At first glance he appears to be the protagonist of a grand cosmic mystery.

That assumption is technically correct.

It is also the fastest way to damage the campaign.

The most important thing to understand is simple:

The story is not about why Lan is special.

The story is about what Lan chooses to do.

The Core Principle

Lan helps people.

That is the foundation of the setting.

Not because he is obligated.

Not because destiny demands it.

Not because a prophecy requires it.

Because he chooses to.

This distinction matters.

The Wrong Way To Run Lan

Do not turn Lan into a galactic messiah.

Do not make every mystery revolve around him.

Do not make every faction obsessed with him.

Do not make every adventure part of a hidden cosmic conspiracy.

Such stories become repetitive quickly.

The Right Way To Run Lan

Lan works best as a catalyst.

He encounters people.

He listens.

He helps.

The stories belong to those encounters.

Ghosts seeking closure.

Students facing impossible choices.

Frontier colonies struggling to survive.

Corporate victims seeking justice.

Lonely monsters trying to remain decent.

Lan provides the opportunity for those stories to happen.

The Beacon Effect

The Beacon Effect exists to generate adventures.

Use it frequently.

Use it creatively.

A ghost may arrive asking for help.

A mimic may seek companionship.

A vampire may need assistance.

A demon may simply want someone willing to listen.

Not every encounter should become combat.

Many should become conversations.

Gray Sense

Gray Sense should create opportunities rather than solutions.

It detects that something is wrong.

It does not automatically reveal what.

Players should still investigate.

Question witnesses.

Search locations.

Solve problems.

Gray Sense starts stories.

It should not end them.

The Archive

The Archive is a library.

Not a cheat code.

Lan cannot instantly know everything.

He cannot replace expertise.

He cannot bypass research.

Treat the Archive as a source of information that still requires effort, interpretation, and understanding.

The Wanderer

The Wanderer serves as the campaign's home base.

Use it.

Encourage scenes aboard the ship.

Meals.

Conversations.

Repairs.

Planning sessions.

Quiet moments.

The hidden floors exist because stories need places where characters can simply live.

The Academy

The Academy should feel alive.

Professors pursue agendas.

Students form relationships.

Corporations recruit talent.

The Order investigates mysteries.

The Academy is not merely a location.

It is a community.

The Corporations

Corporations are not automatically villains.

Some executives are decent people.

Some corporations genuinely improve lives.

Others are predatory.

Treat corporations as institutions composed of individuals.

Complexity creates better stories than caricatures.

The Order

The Radiant Order occupies a similar position.

Most members genuinely want to help.

Some become rigid.

Some become compromised.

Some become heroes.

Some become cautionary tales.

The Order should feel human.

Even when dealing with the supernatural.

The Monsters

Remember one of the setting's most important themes:

Appearance does not determine morality.

A demon may be kind.

An angel may be cruel.

A monster may need help.

A human may be the real threat.

Allow players to discover the truth rather than assuming it.

The Mysteries

Maintain multiple active mysteries.

Some should resolve quickly.

Some should last years.

Some may never receive complete answers.

Mysteries help create a sense of a living universe.

Not every question requires a solution.

Relationships

Relationships are among the most important parts of the campaign.

Friends.

Mentors.

Students.

Ghosts.

Rivals.

Family.

These relationships often matter more than combat encounters.

Invest time in them.

Hope

This setting is dark.

Do not make it hopeless.

The distinction matters.

People suffer.

Terrible things happen.

Corporations exploit.

Ghosts linger.

Monsters exist.

Yet acts of kindness still matter.

Hope remains possible.

That hope is the point.

The Final Rule

Whenever uncertain how to proceed, ask a simple question:

Who needs help?

The answer usually points toward the next adventure.

Lan Gray is not a hero because of the mysteries surrounding him.

He is a hero because he keeps answering that question.

Again.

And again.

And again.

Final Thoughts

Running Lan Gray is ultimately about balancing mystery, horror, wonder, friendship, and hope.

The galaxy is full of powerful people.

Powerful corporations.

Powerful monsters.

Powerful secrets.

Lan's greatest strength is something much simpler.

He cares.

Never lose sight of that.

Everything else tends to work itself out.
